# Just Ask for Calibration: Strategies for Eliciting Calibrated Confidence Scores from Language Models

**Section:** 7.5 — Calibration and Verbalized Confidence

**Authors:** Katherine Tian et al. (Stanford)

**Paper:** [aclanthology.org/2023.emnlp-main.330](https://aclanthology.org/2023.emnlp-main.330)

**Venue:** EMNLP 2023

---

## Why This Paper Is Essential

Published at **our target venue (EMNLP)**. Contains a finding that is potentially threatening to our distributional hypothesis: **verbalized confidence is often better-calibrated than conditional token probabilities for RLHF models**, reducing ECE by ~50%.

---

## Key Findings

1. **Verbalized confidence beats logprobs for RLHF models:** When you simply ask the model "How confident are you? Rate 1-10," the resulting confidence score is better calibrated than the conditional token probability of the answer.

2. **ECE reduction of ~50%:** Verbalized confidence reduces Expected Calibration Error by roughly half compared to logprob-based confidence.

3. **Key insight:** RLHF distorts logprob calibration while **preserving metacognitive text ability.** The model's ability to assess its own confidence *in words* survives RLHF training better than the calibration of its *logprobs*.

---

## Critical Implication for Our Work

This paper is the **strongest threat** to the distributional hypothesis:

- If verbalized confidence (text-only, no logprobs) provides better-calibrated uncertainty estimates than logprobs...
- ...then the text-enriched baseline (Condition D), which includes verbalized confidence, may be a more effective approach than our DAL (Condition E), which relies on logprobs.

**We must address this head-on.** The experimental design already includes Condition D (text-enriched GEPA) as a critical control specifically for this reason.

---

## How We Address This Threat

1. **Condition D vs. Condition E comparison:** The core experiment directly tests whether logprob-based distributional features provide value beyond text-based verbalized confidence.

2. **Different information channels:** Verbalized confidence provides a single aggregate score ("I'm 7/10 confident"). Our DAL provides:
   - **Per-token** uncertainty (which specific step was uncertain)
   - **Competing alternatives** (what the model almost said instead)
   - **Trajectory shape** (how confidence evolved across reasoning steps)

   Even if verbalized confidence is better calibrated as a single number, our features may provide richer diagnostic information.

3. **Semantic vs. numerical:** This paper's finding actually supports our H3 — if text-based confidence is better calibrated, then **semantic** representations of distributional information may also outperform **numerical** representations. Our semantic-first DAL format aligns with this.

## Connection to Other Papers

- **Language Models Know What They Know (7.5.1):** Foundational work on P(True); this paper extends to verbalized confidence
- **Self-Consistency (7.4.1):** Another text-only uncertainty method for Condition D
- **Calibrating Verbalized Confidence (7.5.3):** Improves verbalized confidence further with self-generated distractors
- **Entropy Trajectory Shape (7.2.1):** Trajectory information is available from logprobs but NOT from verbalized confidence — a potential advantage for the DAL

---

## Key Design Implications

- The DAL should emphasize features that **verbalized confidence cannot provide**: per-step trajectory, competing token identities, forking token positions
- If the ablation shows that aggregate confidence features don't help but trajectory/forking features do, it validates the DAL's unique contribution
- The DAL's semantic-first format (not raw numbers) aligns with this paper's finding that text-based metacognition is well-preserved
