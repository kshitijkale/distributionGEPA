# Discovering Latent Knowledge in Language Models Without Supervision (CCS)

**Section:** 7.6 — Contrastive and Distribution-Based Methods

**Authors:** Collin Burns et al.

**Paper:** [arXiv:2212.03827](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.03827)

**Venue:** ICLR 2023

---

## Why This Paper Is Relevant

CCS demonstrates that **hidden states encode truth even when outputs are misleading.** This motivates distributional diagnostics for detecting cases where the model "knows" the right answer but the prompt elicits the wrong one — precisely the failure mode that better prompts should fix.

---

## Key Concept: Contrast-Consistent Search

1. For a yes/no question, extract hidden state representations for both "yes" and "no" continuations
2. Train a probe that satisfies two constraints:
   - **Consistency:** The probe's predictions for "yes" and "no" should be complementary (sum to ~1)
   - **Informativeness:** The probe shouldn't always predict the same thing
3. The probe discovers the model's internal "belief" about the truth, even without labeled data

---

## Key Finding

Hidden states encode truth that the sampled text may not express. A model may output "Paris" while its hidden states suggest it "knows" the answer might be "London" — a disconnect between surface output and internal representation.

---

## How We Use It in Distributional GEPA

1. **Motivates distributional diagnostics:** If the model's internal state encodes information not expressed in text, then distributional features (which are derived from the model's internal state via the output layer) can surface this hidden information.

2. **The "right answer, wrong prompt" failure mode:** CCS's finding is directly relevant to prompt optimization. If the model has the knowledge but the current prompt doesn't elicit it, distributional signals (high entropy, competing correct and incorrect answers) can diagnose this. The reflector can then propose prompt changes that better elicit the model's existing knowledge.

3. **Theoretical foundation:** CCS provides theoretical support for the distributional information gap hypothesis (H1): there exists information in the model's representations that is not expressed in the sampled text output.

---

## Connection to Other Papers

- **Semantic Entropy Probes (7.3.3):** Both use probes on hidden states; CCS for truth, SEP for uncertainty
- **DoLa (7.6.2):** Both access internal representations; DoLa through layer contrasts, CCS through probes
- **Language Models Know What They Know (7.5.1):** Both demonstrate models have internal knowledge beyond their outputs

---

## Limitations for Our Context

- CCS requires hidden state access (more restrictive than logprobs)
- The probe training requires a specific question format (yes/no or A/B)
- Our primary approach uses logprobs, not hidden states — CCS is conceptual motivation, not a direct design input
