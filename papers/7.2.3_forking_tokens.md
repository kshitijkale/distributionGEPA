# Token-Level Entropy Patterns in LLM Reasoning (Forking Tokens)

**Section:** 7.2 — Uncertainty in Chain-of-Thought Reasoning

**Authors:** Wang et al. (2025)

**Source:** Emergent Mind topic synthesis + underlying papers

**Venue:** 2025

---

## Why This Paper Is Critical

This paper directly informs the design of the Distributional Annotation Layer (DAL). It identifies which tokens actually matter for reasoning quality and provides the rationale for focusing annotation on high-entropy "forking tokens" rather than all tokens.

---

## Key Findings

1. **>50% of CoT tokens have near-zero entropy:** Most tokens in a chain-of-thought are deterministic — they are syntactic connectors, repeated phrases, or predictable continuations. These carry no diagnostic information.

2. **~20% are high-entropy "forking tokens":** These occur at logical branching points where the model must make a decision (which fact to reference, which operation to apply, which conclusion to draw). These are the tokens that matter.

3. **Training on forking tokens only is more effective:** Reinforcement learning from verifiable rewards (RLVR) applied only to forking tokens yields **+7.7 on AIME'24** compared to full-token training. This demonstrates that focusing on decision points, not all tokens, produces better learning signal.

---

## Core Concept: Forking Tokens

A "forking token" is a token position where:
- Shannon entropy H_t exceeds a threshold (approximately the top 20% of entropy values in the sequence)
- The model faces a genuine choice between semantically different continuations
- The decision at this point affects downstream reasoning

Examples:
- Choosing which document to reference: "the **second** / **third** / **first** document"
- Selecting an operation: "we should **add** / **multiply** / **subtract**"
- Making a factual claim: "the capital is **Paris** / **London** / **Berlin**"

---

## How We Use It in Distributional GEPA

1. **DAL design — focus on forking tokens:** The annotation layer does NOT report entropy for every token. It identifies forking tokens (top ~20% by entropy) and annotates only those. This keeps diagnostic reports concise and focused.

2. **Competing token identities:** For each forking token, the DAL reports the **identities** of the top-3 competing tokens and their semantic meaning, not just probabilities. E.g., "the model was torn between 'second' and 'third' document references" rather than "H=2.31 bits."

3. **Threshold calibration:** The ~20% figure provides a starting point for adaptive thresholding. We use percentile-based thresholds (top 20% of entropy values per sequence) rather than fixed-bit thresholds, since entropy distributions vary by model and task.

4. **Efficiency argument:** Since >50% of tokens are deterministic, computing and reporting entropy for all tokens is wasteful. Focusing on forking tokens makes the DAL lightweight.

## Connection to Other Papers

- **Entropy Trajectory Shape (7.2.1):** Trajectory analysis operates at the step level; forking token analysis operates at the token level. They are complementary granularities.
- **Entropy Minimization (7.2.2):** EM-INF likely has its biggest effect at forking tokens, even though it operates on all tokens.
- **Entropy-Guided Loop (7.2.4):** Also uses token-level entropy but combines it into aggregate metrics rather than identifying individual forking tokens.
- **Beyond Next Token Probabilities (7.6.4):** The distribution shape at forking tokens matters — a flat distribution (many competitors) is different from a bimodal one (two strong candidates).

---

## Implications for Experimental Design

- **Ablation on granularity (Phase 3, Ablation 2):** Per-token reporting vs. per-step summaries — the forking token concept suggests per-token is valuable but only for the ~20% that are forking tokens.
- **Feature selection in DAL:** Competing token identities at forking tokens may be the single most valuable distributional feature. The ablation should test this independently.

---

## Open Questions

- The 20% figure is an average — what is the variance across tasks, models, and prompt styles?
- How sensitive is the RLVR result to the threshold? Is there a sweet spot between too few (miss important decisions) and too many (dilute signal)?
- Can the reflector LLM use forking token information more effectively than aggregated step-level entropy? This is an empirical question for our ablations.
